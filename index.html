Primary research · independent · 2025–present

Belief-State Geometry
in Small Transformers

Does the belief-state geometry a transformer encodes actually drive its computation — or is it merely legible to us?

Trained on the Mess3 hidden-Markov process, a transformer should — if Shai et al. (2024) generalises — linearly represent a probability distribution over the process's hidden states inside its residual stream. I recover that belief simplex, pin down when it forms, and stress-test whether it is causally load-bearing.

F1

Near-perfect linear decodability

A small transformer represents the full belief-state geometry with R² ≈ 0.99, faithfully recovering the mixed-state fractal and beating every Markov-k baseline. The recovery shown above sits at R² = 0.988.

F2

Emergence, not lookup

The geometry appears during generalization but collapses under memorization — even while loss stays near-optimal. The representation is tied to optimal prediction, not to a memorized lookup table.

F3

Holographic, low-variance encoding

Belief is distributed across ~230 / 256 residual dimensions, and the probe reads it from low-variance directions roughly 90° from the top-PCA / predictive subspace. The signal is everywhere, and almost orthogonal to where the variance lives.

F4

Causally intractable to linear intervention

Because the code is so distributed, linear causal tests are inconclusive: ablation is variance-confounded and activation patching saturates — a matched random-direction control tracks it step for step.

F5

The net result

Belief geometry here is richly decodable, emergence-linked, and confirmed in post-LayerNorm activations — yet causally intractable to linear intervention. As much a finding about the limits of probing-plus-ablation as about the model itself.

Why it matters for safety

If a model's internal world-model is decodable but not linearly steerable, then probe-and-ablate pipelines can over- or under-state what a model is really using. Knowing where that gap lies is a prerequisite for trustworthy interpretability-based oversight.

Extending Shai et al. (2024): how do belief-state representations behave under anomalous / OOD inputs — degrade gracefully, collapse, or drift?
